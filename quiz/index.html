The Depression Quiz — What Every Question Measures

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire) is the most widely used depression screening tool in clinical practice. Here's what each question actually measures and why it matters.

How it works: Each question is scored 0–3 (Not at all / Several days / More than half the days / Nearly every day). Total score ranges from 0 to 27.

The 9 Questions Explained

Q1
Little interest or pleasure in doing things
Measures anhedonia — the inability to feel pleasure. One of the two core symptoms of depression.
Q2
Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless
Measures depressed mood — the other core symptom. Hopelessness specifically predicts severity.
Q3
Trouble falling or staying asleep, or sleeping too much
Sleep disturbance is both a symptom and amplifier of depression. Hypersomnia (too much sleep) is as significant as insomnia.
Q4
Feeling tired or having little energy
Measures psychomotor fatigue — the body-level exhaustion that depression creates, distinct from sleepiness.
Q5
Poor appetite or overeating
Appetite changes signal disruption in the dopamine reward system — the same system affected by depression.
Q6
Feeling bad about yourself or that you are a failure
Measures negative self-concept and guilt — two cognitive hallmarks of major depression.
Q7
Trouble concentrating on things
Depression significantly impairs working memory and executive function. This is often underreported.
Q8
Moving or speaking slowly — or being fidgety and restless
Psychomotor changes (slowing or agitation) are observable physical signs that others may notice before the person does.
Q9
Thoughts of being better off dead or hurting yourself
Suicidal ideation. Any score above 0 here warrants immediate attention regardless of total score.

Scoring Guide

0–4
Minimal
No significant symptoms. Healthy baseline.
5–9
Mild
Worth monitoring. Preventive action recommended.
10–14
Moderate
Significantly affecting daily function. Consider support.
15–19
Mod. Severe
Professional help strongly recommended.
20–27
Severe
Please reach out to a mental health professional or crisis line immediately.

What the PHQ-9 Doesn't Catch

The PHQ-9 measures the frequency of symptoms over 2 weeks. It doesn't account for trauma history, bipolar features, seasonal patterns, or situational factors. A high score is a signal — not a verdict. It tells you to take action, not to conclude anything permanent about yourself.
